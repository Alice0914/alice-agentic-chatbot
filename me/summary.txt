project 0:AI Trend 1-Minute
App explanation: AI Trend 1-Minute is a bilingual (English/Korean) web app that lets you catch the day's most important AI news in about a minute and share it to social media instantly. It auto-collects from nine sources — Anthropic, OpenAI, xAI, NVIDIA, MIT, TechCrunch and others — then runs a four-stage deduplication pipeline (embedding → LLM extraction → logical verification → cross-encoder) to keep only one article per story. A tournament-style scoring loop using Gemini 2.5 Pro and Grok 4.1 assigns each article a 0–100 impact score, so the must-read stories surface at the top of the feed, while top articles are auto-tagged, summarized, translated into Korean, and uploaded to Firestore. The React frontend lets users quickly filter by category, service, or core element and bookmark what they like. One-click sharing to Threads, X, LinkedIn, Reddit, and WhatsApp turns reading into sharing in seconds — from headline to post in under a minute.

— End-to-End Process
A bilingual (English/Korean) AI news curation app. A Python pipeline runs on a daily schedule to collect, deduplicate, score, tag, translate, and upload AI news to Firestore. A React frontend then serves that data as a personalized, one-minute readable feed.

Step 1 — Parallel Collection
The system collects AI news concurrently from multiple sources including AI labs, tech journalism websites, and AI news aggregators.
All collected articles are normalized into a unified structure containing the title, content, source, URL, and publication date for downstream processing.
The pipeline also performs content cleaning, metadata extraction, duplicate detection, and source prioritization. To improve reliability, each source is assigned an authority score, which is later used to resolve overlapping or duplicated news articles across different platforms.
This helps create a cleaner and more trustworthy dataset for AI summarization, ranking, and recommendation workflows.

Step 2 — Four-Stage Deduplication
Because the same announcement is usually covered by multiple sources, the pipeline runs a four-stage dedup that balances recall (catching every duplicate) with precision (not collapsing unrelated stories that happen to share keywords). Stage 1 is a cheap fan-out: gemini-embedding-2-preview produces 768-dim title embeddings and any pair with cosine similarity above 0.92 becomes a candidate. Stage 2 sends the candidate pair's full content to gemini-2.5-flash, which extracts a structured record of {entities, event_type, product_name, date}. Stage 3 then applies a rule-based check that requires at least two of those four fields to match — this is what filters out look-alike but unrelated stories. Stage 4 confirms the survivors with a cross-encoder/stsb-roberta-large reranker, requiring a final score above 0.90. When a duplicate is confirmed, the article from the higher-priority source is kept and its exposure_score is incremented by one for each duplicate dropped — a signal that the whole industry is talking about this story.

Step 3 — Tournament Scoring and Tagging
Surviving articles are then run through a tournament-style scoring process that escalates compute only for promising candidates. Round 1 always runs gemini-2.5-pro for an initial 1–5 rating with reasoning. If that score is at least 3.0, Round 2 fetches an independent second opinion from xAI's grok-4-1-fast-reasoning. If the average of Rounds 1 and 2 is at least 4.0, Round 3 does a final verification pass with gemini-2.5-pro to confirm top-tier candidates. The mean star rating is multiplied by 20 to land on a 0–100 scale, then exposure_score * 2 is added as a bonus and the result is capped at 100. After scoring, articles are sorted by impactScore and passed to a gemini-2.5-flash tagging step that produces a clean title, a 2–3 sentence summary, three to five search keywords, and strict-allowlist tags for categories, productServices, and coreElements. To control cost, a why_it_matters blurb is generated only for the top 20 articles.

Step 4 — Korean Translation and Final JSON
The translator stage runs in backend/agents/processors/translator.py and combines two strategies. LLM translation via gemini-2.5-flash handles the free-text fields — title, summary, why_it_matters (when present), and searchKeywords — producing their *_ko counterparts with a prompt that keeps widely-used technical terms like "AI", "LLM", and "GPT" in English. The classification tags use deterministic map-based translation instead, looking up categories_ko, productServices_ko, and coreElements_ko directly from CATEGORY_MAP, SERVICE_MAP, and CORE_MAP in config.py — no LLM call needed, so the result is consistent across runs. A URL-safe id is generated by slugifying the title and appending the date, and publishedDate is normalized to YYYY-MM-DD. The full per-day output is saved to backend/data/final_data/final_news_output_<YYYYMMDD>.json.

Step 5 — Firestore Upload
The uploader appends each finalized article to the news_feeds/{YYYY-MM} document, storing the day's articles inside a news array field. The pipeline is meant to run on a smart schedule: on Mondays it pulls Saturday and Sunday together, on Tuesday through Saturday it pulls yesterday only, and on Sundays it doesn't run at all.

Step 6 — Frontend Delivery
The React app in src/pages/UserApp.jsx handles everything user-facing. Users sign in through Firebase Auth (Google, email, Twitter, or LinkedIn), then go through an onboarding flow where they select their preferred categories, services, and core elements. The feed view queries news_feeds/{YYYY-MM}, filters by date and the user's interests, and renders cards in impactScore order. i18next picks either the English fields or the *_ko fields depending on the user's language, so the same Firestore document drives both locales without a separate fetch. From there users can filter further, bookmark, like, and one-click share to Threads, X, LinkedIn, Reddit, or WhatsApp — the whole flow designed to surface the day's important AI news in about a minute.

TL;DR: Collect from nine sources → 4-stage dedup → tournament scoring + tagging → Korean translation → Firestore upload → React feed with filtering and sharing.

project 1: SmartPlanner — App Overview
What it is
SmartPlanner is an AI-powered daily planner built around metacognition. Most planners only let you write down what you intend to do. SmartPlanner is different: every 15/30/60-minute slot has both a Planned field and an Actual field, side by side. You see in one glance the gap between what you said you would do and what you actually did — and that gap is where self-awareness grows.

This "plan vs. actual" pairing is the foundation of the entire app. The AI coaching, weekly habit tracker, and efficiency metrics are all just structured reflections on that gap, designed to help you build accurate self-knowledge instead of vague guilt.

The Core Idea: Metacognition Loop

PLAN  →  DO  →  SEE THE GAP  →  AI REFLECTS WITH YOU  →  ADJUST  →  REPEAT
Plan — block out your day in 30-min slots (morning, what you intend)
Do — fill in the "actual" field as the day unfolds (or at end of day)
See the gap — planned vs actual sit next to each other; you can't avoid noticing what slipped
AI reflects — Gemini analyzes the gap and writes you a 3-4 sentence coaching message the next morning
Adjust — the gap, named and explained, becomes data instead of shame
This loop is what builds 메타인지 (metacognition) — knowing what you actually do, not what you imagine you do.

Core Features
Side-by-side Planned vs Actual schedule — the metacognitive heart of the app; visible mismatch every day
Two-way Google Calendar sync — edits in the planner push to GCal; GCal events pull back in
AI habit detection — Gemini decides which tasks are routines (Stretching, Reading) vs goals (Work, Project X)
Weekly Habits Tracker — surfaces tasks you've repeated 3+ days that the AI flagged as habits
Efficiency Metrics — planned vs. actual completion per category, switchable between task counts and hours (this is the gap, quantified)
Time Distribution donut — visualizes your week across Productive / Maintenance / Recovery domains
AI coaching feedback — daily morning message + weekly Sunday review, written like a behavioral science coach

####################Review Resume######################
When crafting or evaluating an AI/Data Science résumé, it is essential to organize your content around four foundational pillars—Technical Competencies, Job Fit, Project Quality, and Emerging Trends—while also tailoring every detail to the specific work scope and skill requirements of the target role.

First, your résumé must clearly demonstrate core technical competencies. Begin by distinguishing between your proficiency in using existing tools and frameworks and your ability to develop reusable solutions of your own. For example, instead of simply stating “Python expertise,” describe how you designed and published three internal data-processing Python packages, and contrast that with leveraging pandas and PySpark for large-scale ETL tasks. In the SQL section, highlight advanced techniques—window functions, complex joins, or execution-plan optimizations—and quantify your impact, such as “reduced query runtime by 60% through index tuning.” Modern technology stacks deserve similar treatment: explain why you chose to learn a new tool—be it an LLM framework, an AutoML platform, or an MLOps pipeline—and how you applied it in a real project, emphasizing both your motivation and outcomes. Close this pillar by detailing your data-processing workflow—from exploratory data analysis and feature engineering to outlier detection and missing-value strategies—and linking these steps to concrete business insights. Finally, illustrate your statistical and machine-learning expertise with specifics: describe hypothesis tests, model-training processes, hyperparameter tuning, and ensemble methods, and show “before and after” improvements (for instance, “improved classification accuracy from 85% to 91%”).

Second, strong candidates align their backgrounds with the job fit criteria of the role they’re pursuing. Recruiters will look for domain expertise that matches their team’s focus: if time-series forecasting is central, lead with your forecasting projects and their performance metrics (e.g., “achieved a weekly forecast error below 5%”). Demonstrate industry and business acumen by describing how you solved real-world problems in finance, healthcare, manufacturing, or whichever sector applies, and articulate what you learned about its data challenges. Most importantly, quantify your contributions in terms of ROI or key performance indicators: “recommended process changes that increased production by 15%, generating $1 million in incremental revenue.”

The third pillar, project quality, highlights your problem-solving approach and end-to-end ownership. Avoid generic tutorials or toy datasets; instead, document the full decision-making lifecycle—defining the problem, selecting and justifying methods, and interpreting results. Showcase your experience deploying models into production: discuss containerization (Docker/Kubernetes), CI/CD integrations, monitoring strategies, and use of collaboration tools such as Git, issue trackers, or Agile workflows. Demonstrating that you can carry a project from data ingestion through deployment and ongoing maintenance is a powerful differentiator.

Fourth, reflecting 2025’s AI and data trends signals that you’re at the forefront of the field. Describe your work with generative AI or autonomous (“agentic”) pipelines, including prompt engineering or LLM orchestration. Outline any ethical-AI or governance frameworks you’ve implemented to ensure fairness and transparency. Detail your MLOps and infrastructure experience on cloud platforms—AWS, GCP, or Azure—including model versioning, autoscaling, and uptime metrics (e.g., “maintained 99.9% model availability”). If you have used AutoML or explainable-AI tools, briefly note the effect on development velocity or stakeholder trust.

Throughout your résumé, avoid red flags—vague achievements, mere lists of technologies, or projects lacking clear business impact—and instead cultivate green flags: measurable KPIs, deep role alignment, and evidence of continuous learning (certifications, conference talks, open-source contributions). For entry-level candidates, emphasize personal projects, course certificates, and the “I’ve prepared diligently” narrative; for experienced professionals, lead with leadership examples, cross-functional impact, and high-value business results.

Finally, even roles sharing the same title can differ significantly in work scope and required skills. Before submission, extract key terms from the job description—whether real-time streaming versus batch analytics or PySpark versus TensorFlow—and reorder your résumé so that projects explicitly showcasing those skills appear first. Less directly relevant experiences can follow in supporting sections. By weaving these four pillars into a cohesive narrative and tailoring your résumé to each opportunity’s unique demands, you will present a compelling, targeted case for why you are the ideal candidate.
